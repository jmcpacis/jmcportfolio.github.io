Week 9 of MSHI

Some patients keep copies of their lab results and prescriptions. Some leave their lab results with their physicians and don’t keep a copy. These lab results and prescriptions are often in paper format. Having such health information at hand is vital to helping patients manage their conditions, especially for those with chronic disease.

This week’s driving question invites you take a look at electronic health records, but this time in the shoes of the patient.

Overview

Personal health records (PHRs) should be designed to seamlessly integrate and exchange data with electronic health records (EHRs) used by healthcare providers. This interoperability is crucial for ensuring a comprehensive view of the patient's health history. Standardizing data formats and coding within PHRs and EHRs promotes interoperability, allowing for the exchange of information without loss of meaning or context.

A well-designed PHR should support bidirectional data flow, enabling patients to receive updates from their healthcare providers and allowing providers to access relevant information entered by patients.


Patients may face challenges in maintaining accurate and complete information in their PHRs, which can affect the reliability of the data available to healthcare providers. Encouraging patients to consistently use and update their PHRs requires effective education and user-friendly interfaces. Low adoption rates can hinder the success of PHR implementations. Integrating PHRs into the existing healthcare workflow poses challenges, as healthcare providers need systems that efficiently incorporate patient-generated data without disrupting their processes.


PHRs provide patients with easy access to a comprehensive record of their health information, including lab results, prescriptions, and medical history, enabling a more informed approach to managing chronic conditions.

1. Patients can use PHRs to track and manage medication schedules, reducing the likelihood of missed doses and promoting adherence to prescribed treatment plans.
2. PHRs allow patients to monitor trends in their health over time, facilitating early detection of changes or patterns related to their chronic conditions.
3. Patients can share relevant information from their PHRs with healthcare providers, fostering better communication and collaboration in the management of chronic diseases.
4. PHRs can be a tool for patient education, providing resources and information that empower individuals to actively participate in their care and make informed decisions about lifestyle and treatment choices.
