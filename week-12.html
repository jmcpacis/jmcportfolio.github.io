Overview

Telemedicine, or the use of information and communication technologies (ICT) to deliver health care services over distance, has been widely advocated as a potential solution to improve health access, quality, and equity, especially in developing countries where health systems face many constraints. However, telemedicine also poses many challenges and barriers that need to be overcome to ensure its successful implementation and sustainability. In this blog post, we will explore how telemedicine projects can be better designed in low-resource settings, and why telemedicine has not become mainstream in the Philippines, a lower-middle income country in Southeast Asia.

Telemedicine projects are complex and multidimensional interventions that involve various technical, organizational, social, and ethical aspects. Therefore, they require careful and comprehensive design and evaluation to ensure their feasibility, acceptability, effectiveness, and impact. According to Combi et al. (2016), some of the key design issues for telemedicine projects in developing countries are:
1. Technology selection: Designing a telemedicine project is to select the appropriate ICT tools and platforms that can support the delivery of the health services, considering the availability, affordability, reliability, and usability of the technology. This can be done by conducting a technology assessment, a cost-benefit analysis, a risk analysis, and a user satisfaction analysis, among other methods.
2. Evaluation framework: The need to establish the evaluation framework, or the way the telemedicine project is monitored and measured, considering the objectives, indicators, methods, and tools of the evaluation. This can be done by conducting a logic model, a theory of change, a performance measurement framework, and an impact evaluation, among other methods.

The Philippines is a lower-middle income country with a population of more than 100 million people, spread over more than 7,000 islands. The country faces many health challenges, such as high maternal and child mortality, high burden of infectious and chronic diseases, low health workforce density, and inequitable distribution of health resources. Telemedicine has been proposed as a way to improve the access and quality of health care in the country, especially for the rural and underserved areas.

The Philippines lacks a clear and comprehensive policy and regulatory framework for telemedicine, which creates uncertainty and confusion among the stakeholders, and hinders the integration and standardization of telemedicine in the health system. For example, there are no clear guidelines on the licensure, accreditation, reimbursement, liability, and ethics of telemedicine practice in the country.

The Philippines has a low and uneven ICT infrastructure and technology penetration, which limits the availability and reliability of telemedicine services, especially in the remote and rural areas. For example, there are frequent power outages, internet disruptions, and network congestion, which affect the quality and continuity of telemedicine services.

The Philippines has a low and variable capacity and skills of the health workers and the patients to use telemedicine, which affects the acceptability and effectiveness of telemedicine services, especially in the low-literacy and low-awareness settings. For example, there are gaps in the training, education, and support of the health workers and the patients on how to use telemedicine, and how to deal with the technical, clinical, and social issues that may arise from telemedicine practice.

Telemedicine is a promising and innovative intervention that can improve the health outcomes and access to health services, especially in developing countries where health systems face many constraints. However, telemedicine also faces many challenges and barriers that need to be addressed to ensure its successful implementation and sustainability. By conducting a needs assessment, a technology selection, a service delivery model, and an evaluation framework, telemedicine projects can be better designed in low-resource settings. By addressing the policy and regulation, the infrastructure and technology, the capacity and skills, and the culture and behavior barriers, telemedicine can become mainstream in the Philippines.

References 1. Combi C, Pozzani G, Pozzi G. Telemedicine for developing countries: a survey and some design issues. Appl Clin Inform 2016; 7: 1025–1050

2. Macabasag RL et al., Implementation of Telemedicine Services in Lower-Middle Income Countries: Lessons for the Philippines. J Int Soc Telemed eHealth 2016;4:e24
